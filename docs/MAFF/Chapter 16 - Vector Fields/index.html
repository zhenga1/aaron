Introduction

Information is all around us. For example, sometimes, different people may own different things, and may have different hobbies, etc. In practice, such information is pretty hard to mathematically model. But, when such information includes both a magnitude and a direction, we can sometimes use a math tool to help us, ==vectors.==

In the 2D world, the application of such tool can be seen in the description of gradient fields of altitude curves. For example, each point on the vector field corresponds to a vector which tells of the direction of steepest ascent, and the slope of the ascent.

In the 3D world, the application of such tools can be seen in the description of certain fields, most notably the electromagnetic, gravitational, or other force fields. In such cases, for each point in the field, we can draw a vector with a magnitude and direction, representing the exact magnitude and direction of the applied force(on a specific object) at that point. Another example could be a velocity field, which shows object's velocities at different points along a 3D3D3D space. Such fields are what we call vector fields

![[Acrobat_F41zqqjobt.png]] ![[Acrobat_272uW0bVzN.png]]

Essentially, a ==vector field== is any function F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z) that sends any point in the nnn-dimensional plane(x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z), (3D3D3D is shown in this definition) within a domain EEE to an nnn dimensional vector in RnR^nRn

For ease of understanding and analysis, one can express a vector field F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z) as the sum of its components dotted with the unit component vectors, such as (i,j,k)(i,j,k)(i,j,k). F(x,y,z)=P(x,y,z)i+Q(x,y,z)j+R(x,y,z)kF(x,y,z) = P(x,y,z)i + Q(x,y,z)j + R(x,y,z)kF(x,y,z)=P(x,y,z)i+Q(x,y,z)j+R(x,y,z)k

A gradient vector field F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z) is any vector field that can be expressed as the gradient vector of another function f(x,y,z)f(x,y,z)f(x,y,z) within a certain domain, as such:

Curl and Divergence

For any given vector field F=Pi+Qj+RkF = Pi + Qj + RkF=Pi+Qj+Rk in 3D space, the curl of the vector field is defined as a vector that represents the cross product between the del/gradient operator ∇\nabla∇, and the vector field itself. In other words, the curl of a vector field is described by the following: curl(F)=[ijkddxddyddzPQR]curl (F) = \begin{bmatrix}i&&j&&k\\\frac{d}{dx}&&\frac{d}{dy}&&\frac{d}{dz}\\P&&Q&&R\end{bmatrix}curl(F)=⎣⎢⎡​idxd​P​​jdyd​Q​​kdzd​R​⎦⎥⎤​

Essentially, the curl of a vector field FFF has the geometric meaning of representing the magnitude and (axial) direction to which a vector field rotates. In a sense, at a point (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) defined on the vector field FFF, the direction of the curl vector tells you the exact axis by which the vector field will rotate the point (counterclockwise when looking from the top of the axis).

Essentially, if you were to take an infinitesimally sized 3D cube at the point (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) and leave it in a force field with magnitudes and directions at all points governed by FFF , the direction of the curl tells you the direction in which the cube will rotate, anticlockwisely, and the magnitude of the curl tells you the extent of the rotation, sort of like an analog to the rotational speed. ![[Pasted image 20221124010707.png]] Similarly, for any given point (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) on the vector field F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z)F(x,y,z), the divergence is a scalar quantity that represents the dot product between the del/gradient operator ∇\nabla∇, and the vector field itself. It is denoted by the expression: divergence(F)=dPdx+dQdy+dRdzdivergence(F) = \frac{dP}{dx} + \frac{dQ}{dy}+\frac{dR}{dz}divergence(F)=dxdP​+dydQ​+dzdR​ This quantity expresses the extent to which the vector field flows in or out of the point. Essentially, if a vector field flows out of a point more than it flows in,then we say the point has positive divergence, and vice versa. ![[Pasted image 20221124010559.png]] Curl and Divergence are very useful concepts because they introduce operations that tell us more about an unknown 3D vector space than we previously would've known using just the gradient operator ∇\nabla∇ itself. The curl parameter tells us about useful things such as the net shear on each axis at every given point caused by the vector field, whilst the divergence tells us, if the vector field is describing the motion of some body, how much new stuff (be it mass, energy or something) is being created by the vector field.

It turns out, however, that this idea can also be extended into 3D.

If we instead let CCC be any closed curve in 3D3D3D space, and let DDD be the region enclosed by CCC, and we suppose that FFF is a vector field in 3D3D3D defined on the entirety of the region DDD, and we let n(x,y,z)n(x,y,z)n(x,y,z) be the unit normal vector to the surface DDD at any point (x,y,z)(x,y,z)(x,y,z) on DDD, then we can write the following:

Stokes theorem: ∫CF⋅dr⃗=∫∫D(curlF)⋅n⃗dA=∫∫D(curlF)⋅dS⃗\int_C F\cdot d\vec{r} = \int\int_D (curl F)\cdot \vec{n} dA = \int\int_D (curlF) \cdot d\vec{S}∫C​F⋅dr=∫∫D​(curlF)⋅ndA=∫∫D​(curlF)⋅dS It turns out the equivalent of Greene's theorem in 3D also is a true statement! With this, it is obvious that Greene's theorem is just a very special case of Stokes theorem, whereby the region DDD is entirely horizontal and orthogonal to the zzz axis at every point.

AMAZING!

We can now also introduce the divergence theorem. Remember that another analog to Greene's theorem can be written as follows: ∫CF⋅n⃗ds=∫∫Ddiv(F(x,y))dA\int_C F\cdot \vec{n} ds = \int\int_D div(F(x,y)) dA∫C​F⋅nds=∫∫D​div(F(x,y))dA Which essentially is telling us that the dot product between a vector field FFF with continuous component partial derivatives integrated along a closed boundary curve CCC is equal to the divergence of the vector field integrated across the region DDD in the xyxyxy plane enclosed by CCC.

If we were to hope to extend this to the 3rd dimension, we would effectively seek to be enclosing a 3D3D3D-region DDD via a surface SSS. So, our 3D analog would be: ∫∫SF⋅n⃗dS=∫∫∫Ddiv(F(x,y,z))dA\int\int_S F\cdot \vec{n} dS = \int\int\int_D div(F(x,y,z)) dA∫∫S​F⋅ndS=∫∫∫D​div(F(x,y,z))dA In fact, this equation above, called the divergence theorem, is true under certain constraints.

Firstly, we see that n⃗\vec{n}n has to be postively oriented, which just means that n⃗\vec{n}n must point away from the surface itself(see below image): ![[chrome_EaH6WlsPj2.png]] Secondly, FFF obviously must have components that each have continuous partial derivatives relative to the axes x,y,zx,y,zx,y,z. within the region defined by DDD, as well as the boundary curve CCC.

Effectively, what this theorem tells us is that, regardless of whether the reference is in 2D2D2D or 3D3D3D, the total divergence(flux) of a vector field FFF within a region DDD is simply the sum of the divergences at the boundary.

With this, we have concluded the analysis of vectors.
